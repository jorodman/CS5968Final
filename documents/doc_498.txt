He was Professor of Medieval History at the University of Leeds from 1924 to 1939. Early life and education [ edit ] Thompson was born on 7 November 1873 at Clifton, Bristol , the son of The Reverend John Thompson, Vicar of St Gabriel's, Bristol, and his wife Annie Hastings ( née Cooper, daughter of Canon David Cooper). He attended Clifton College from 1883 to 1890 and Totnes School for a year. He gained a minor scholarship to read Classics at St John's College, Cambridge from 1892 to 1895. [1] He received his BA in 1895, later promoted to MA in 1903. Academic career [ edit ] After graduating, Thompson tutored in Europe until 1897, when he returned to Cambridge as an extramural teacher; [1] he was a lecturer for the Cambridge University Extension course until 1919. [2] During this period, he began publishing books on English literature, the history and architecture of the English parish church and military architecture in medieval England. [1] Thompson was appointed a lecturer in English at Armstrong College, Newcastle upon Tyne (then part of Durham University , now Newcastle University ) in 1919. He was promoted to Reader in Medieval History and Archaeology in 1921. In 1922, Thompson moved to the University of Leeds , where he was appointed Reader in Medieval History and promoted to Professor two years later. [2] He was head of the Department of History at Leeds from 1927 until his retirement in 1939. [1] During this period, he published studies of buildings and editions of medieval records. [1] Thompson was the Ford Lecturer at the University of Oxford in 1932-33. His subject was The English clergy and their organisation in the later Middle Ages . He was Birkbeck Lecturer at Trinity College, Cambridge , in 1933-35 on The English Church at the Opening of the Sixteenth Century . Thompson was a member of several public bodies responsible for historic buildings. [1] Honours [ edit ] Alongside three honorary doctorates , [1] Thompson was elected a Member of the Royal Archaeological Institute in 1909, [2] a Fellow of the Society of Antiquaries (FSA) in 1910 [1] or 1911, [2] and a Fellow of the British Academy (FBA) in 1928. [1] He was appointed a Commander of the Order of the British Empire (CBE) in 1938 and served as president of the Royal Archaeological Institute from 1939 to 1945. [1] He was the subject of a Festschrift in 1948. [3] Thompson was President of the Leicestershire Archaeological Society from 1937 until his death in 1952. [4] Marriage and children [ edit ] Thompson married Amy Gosling in 1903. They had two children. Death [ edit ] Thompson died in Exmouth on 4 September 1952 at the age of 78. His wife predeceased him in 1945.
